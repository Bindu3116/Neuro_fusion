\vspace{0.5in}

Psychological stress is a pervasive health concern linked to cardiovascular disease, cognitive impairment, and mental health disorders. Accurate, automated stress assessment from physiological signals can enable timely interventions and continuous mental wellness monitoring. However, existing approaches often rely on a single biosignal modality, failing to capture the rich interplay between the central and autonomic nervous systems that characterizes the stress response. This project presents NeuroFusionGPT, a multimodal deep learning framework that fuses electroencephalogram (EEG) and electrocardiogram (ECG) signals through a bidirectional cross-attention mechanism to classify five stress levels: Calm, Mild Stress, Moderate Stress, High Stress, and Severe Stress.

The proposed architecture employs a Transformer encoder for EEG data, treating the 16 EEG channels as a sequence of tokens with sinusoidal positional encoding to capture inter-channel dependencies. ECG data, represented as a 187-dimensional feature vector, is processed through a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with batch normalization. Both modality encoders produce 128-dimensional embeddings that are fused via a bidirectional cross-attention module, where EEG attends to ECG and ECG attends to EEG, enabling the model to learn complementary brain--heart coupling patterns. The fused representation is classified by a two-layer MLP head. Training employs focal loss with inverse-frequency class weights to address severe class imbalance, the AdamW optimizer with learning rate reduction on plateau, and early stopping on the validation macro F1-score.

The framework was trained and evaluated using the Bangalore EEG Epilepsy Dataset (BEED) for EEG features and a publicly available ECG stress dataset, with the ECG stress labels serving as the unified classification target. Experimental results demonstrate that the fusion model achieves a test accuracy of 93.15\%, balanced accuracy of 91.33\%, and macro F1-score of 0.783 over 37 training epochs with the best model selected at epoch 27. Per-class analysis reveals strong performance on Calm (F1: 0.960) and Severe Stress (F1: 0.956) classes, with lower performance on minority classes Mild Stress (F1: 0.501) and High Stress (F1: 0.614), reflecting the inherent class imbalance. An optional application-layer integration with a large language model (LLM) via the OpenRouter API generates personalized wellness feedback from the model's predictions.

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Purpose of the Study}
\label{sec:purpose}

Psychological stress has emerged as one of the most significant public health challenges of the twenty-first century. Chronic stress is associated with a wide range of adverse outcomes including cardiovascular disease, weakened immune function, cognitive decline, anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life~\cite{mcewen2008}. The World Health Organization has identified workplace stress alone as a contributor to an estimated 120,000 deaths annually in the United States and projected global economic costs exceeding \$300 billion per year in lost productivity~\cite{who2022}. Despite the well-established health consequences, accurate and objective assessment of stress levels remains a difficult problem, as subjective self-report instruments such as the Perceived Stress Scale are prone to recall bias, social desirability effects, and limited temporal resolution~\cite{cohen1983}.

Physiological signals offer an objective window into the body's stress response. The electroencephalogram (EEG) captures electrical activity generated by cortical neurons and reflects the central nervous system's response to stress, including changes in alpha, beta, and theta band power that are associated with cognitive load, arousal, and emotional regulation~\cite{alberdi2016}. The electrocardiogram (ECG) measures the electrical activity of the heart and captures autonomic nervous system markers of stress, including heart rate variability (HRV), which reflects the balance between sympathetic and parasympathetic activity~\cite{kim2018}. By combining these two modalities, a stress detection system can capture both the brain's cognitive-emotional processing and the body's autonomic physiological response, providing a more complete picture of the stress state than either modality alone.

The purpose of this project is to design, implement, and evaluate NeuroFusionGPT, a multimodal deep learning framework that fuses EEG and ECG signals through a cross-attention mechanism for five-level stress classification. The framework additionally integrates an optional large language model at the application layer to translate numerical stress predictions into personalized wellness feedback, creating an end-to-end pipeline from raw biosignals to actionable health guidance.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem}

Existing approaches to physiological stress detection suffer from several limitations that this project seeks to address. First, the majority of prior work operates on a single biosignal modality, typically ECG-derived HRV features or EEG power spectral features, and fails to exploit the complementary information available from simultaneous brain and heart monitoring. Single-modality classifiers are inherently limited by the information content of their input; for instance, ECG alone cannot capture the cognitive appraisal component of stress, while EEG alone may miss autonomic changes that manifest primarily in cardiac activity.

Second, approaches that do consider multiple modalities typically employ simple fusion strategies such as early concatenation of feature vectors or late fusion through majority voting of independent classifiers. These methods do not model the interactions between modalities explicitly and therefore cannot learn the complex, bidirectional relationships between brain activity and cardiac function that characterize the stress response. For example, increased cognitive load as measured by EEG may predict a subsequent increase in sympathetic cardiac activation, and elevated heart rate as measured by ECG may feed back to alter cortical arousal patterns. A fusion mechanism that can model such interactions stands to produce a richer and more discriminative joint representation.

Third, stress datasets are characteristically imbalanced, with certain stress levels substantially underrepresented. Standard classification models trained on imbalanced data tend to develop a systematic bias toward majority classes, achieving high overall accuracy while performing poorly on the minority classes that may represent the most clinically significant stress states.

Fourth, most existing systems produce a categorical label as output without providing interpretable, actionable guidance to the end user. A stress classification of ``High Stress'' is meaningful to a clinician but offers limited value to an individual seeking to understand and manage their stress unless accompanied by context-sensitive recommendations.

This project addresses these four challenges by developing a cross-attention-based multimodal fusion architecture, employing focal loss with class weights for imbalance handling, and integrating a large language model to generate personalized wellness feedback.

\section{Scope and Objectives}
\label{sec:scope}

The scope of this project encompasses the complete pipeline from raw EEG and ECG data to stress-level prediction with optional LLM-generated feedback. The specific objectives are as follows.

The first objective is to design and implement modality-specific encoders that transform heterogeneous biosignal inputs into a shared embedding space. The EEG encoder must capture inter-channel dependencies in the 16-channel EEG signal, while the ECG encoder must learn a compact representation of the 187-dimensional ECG feature vector.

The second objective is to develop a bidirectional cross-attention fusion mechanism that learns the interactions between the EEG and ECG embeddings, producing a unified representation that captures brain--heart coupling patterns relevant to stress classification.

The third objective is to train the fusion model for five-class stress classification (Calm, Mild Stress, Moderate Stress, High Stress, Severe Stress) using focal loss with inverse-frequency class weights and evaluate its performance using metrics appropriate for imbalanced multi-class classification, including balanced accuracy, macro F1-score, and per-class precision, recall, and F1-score.

The fourth objective is to integrate the trained model with a large language model through the OpenRouter API to generate natural-language wellness feedback based on the predicted stress level and confidence scores.

\chapter{Background and Literature Review}
\label{ch:background}

This chapter examines the theoretical foundations and prior research informing the design of NeuroFusionGPT. The review covers the physiology of the stress response, biosignal modalities for stress detection, machine learning and deep learning approaches, multimodal fusion strategies, and class imbalance handling techniques.

\section{Physiology of the Stress Response}
\label{sec:stress_physiology}

The human stress response involves a coordinated activation of the central nervous system (CNS) and the autonomic nervous system (ANS). When the brain perceives a stressor, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis initiates a cascade of hormonal and neural changes that prepare the body for action~\cite{mcewen2008}. At the cortical level, stress is associated with decreased alpha-band (8--13 Hz) power, reflecting reduced relaxation, and increased beta-band (13--30 Hz) power, reflecting heightened arousal and cognitive engagement~\cite{alberdi2016}. Frontal asymmetry, the relative difference in alpha power between the left and right frontal regions, has been linked to emotional valence, with rightward asymmetry associated with negative affect and stress~\cite{davidson2004}.

At the cardiac level, stress activates the sympathetic branch of the ANS, increasing heart rate and decreasing heart rate variability. Specific HRV metrics, including the standard deviation of normal-to-normal intervals (SDNN), the root mean square of successive differences (RMSSD), and the low-frequency to high-frequency power ratio (LF/HF), serve as established biomarkers of autonomic balance and stress~\cite{kim2018}. The interplay between cortical and cardiac responses is bidirectional: cognitive appraisal of a stressor triggers autonomic changes, and peripheral physiological states feed back to modulate cortical processing through interoceptive pathways~\cite{critchley2013}.

This bidirectional brain--heart coupling provides the physiological rationale for multimodal fusion of EEG and ECG in stress detection. A model that can learn the co-variation patterns between cortical and cardiac signals is theoretically positioned to achieve more accurate and robust stress classification than one operating on either modality in isolation.

\section{EEG-Based Stress Detection}
\label{sec:eeg_stress}

Electroencephalography records the summated postsynaptic potentials of cortical neurons through electrodes placed on the scalp according to standardized montages such as the International 10-20 system~\cite{jasper1958}. EEG signals are characterized by frequency bands: delta (0.5--4 Hz), theta (4--8 Hz), alpha (8--13 Hz), beta (13--30 Hz), and gamma (30--100 Hz). Stress-related changes in EEG have been documented across multiple bands. Subhani et al.~\cite{subhani2017} reported that stressed subjects exhibited significantly higher beta power and lower alpha power compared to relaxed subjects, consistent with increased cortical arousal. Al-Shargie et al.~\cite{alshargie2016} demonstrated that frontal theta-beta ratios could distinguish stressed from non-stressed states with accuracies exceeding 85\% using support vector machines.

More recently, deep learning approaches have been applied to EEG-based stress detection. Arpaia et al.~\cite{arpaia2022} used convolutional neural networks on EEG spectrograms to classify stress levels, achieving accuracy of 89.7\% on a two-class problem. Li et al.~\cite{li2022} applied an attention-based recurrent neural network to EEG time series and showed that the attention mechanism could identify stress-relevant temporal patterns in the signal. These studies demonstrate the feasibility of EEG-based stress detection but also highlight the challenge of multi-class stress-level discrimination, which remains considerably more difficult than binary stress/no-stress classification.

\section{ECG-Based Stress Detection}
\label{sec:ecg_stress}

The electrocardiogram captures the electrical activity of the heart and provides a rich set of features for stress assessment. Time-domain HRV features (SDNN, RMSSD, pNN50), frequency-domain features (LF power, HF power, LF/HF ratio), and non-linear features (sample entropy, detrended fluctuation analysis) have all been used as inputs to stress classifiers~\cite{kim2018}. Healey and Picard~\cite{healey2005} conducted a landmark study demonstrating that ECG-derived HRV features could classify driver stress into three levels with 97\% accuracy using linear discriminant analysis. Subsequent work by Gjoreski et al.~\cite{gjoreski2017} applied random forests to a comprehensive set of HRV features for continuous stress monitoring, achieving 92\% accuracy in a binary setting.

Deep learning approaches to ECG-based stress detection have emerged more recently. Sarkar and Etemad~\cite{sarkar2020} proposed a self-supervised learning approach for ECG-based stress detection using contrastive learning and achieved state-of-the-art results on the WESAD dataset. The ECG stress dataset used in this project provides 187 pre-extracted features for each heartbeat record across five stress levels, offering a richer feature space than raw waveform approaches while preserving clinically meaningful information.

\section{Multimodal Fusion for Biosignal Classification}
\label{sec:multimodal}

Multimodal fusion combines information from multiple data sources to improve classification performance beyond what any single modality can achieve. Fusion strategies are generally categorized as early fusion (feature-level), late fusion (decision-level), and intermediate or hybrid fusion~\cite{baltrusaitis2019}.

Early fusion concatenates features from all modalities into a single feature vector before classification. While simple to implement, this approach treats modalities as independent and does not model cross-modal interactions. Furthermore, when modalities have very different dimensionalities or statistical properties, concatenation can lead to one modality dominating the learned representation.

Late fusion trains independent classifiers for each modality and combines their predictions, typically through voting or averaging. This approach preserves modality-specific representations but cannot learn cross-modal patterns that emerge from the interaction between modalities.

Attention-based fusion has emerged as a powerful intermediate approach that explicitly models cross-modal interactions. The multi-head attention mechanism, introduced by Vaswani et al.~\cite{vaswani2017} in the Transformer architecture, allows one modality to attend to another, computing a weighted combination of the attended modality's representation where the weights reflect relevance to the querying modality. Cross-attention, where the query comes from one modality and the key-value pairs come from another, has been successfully applied to vision-language tasks~\cite{lu2019} and audio-visual tasks~\cite{tsai2019}.

In the biosignal domain, multimodal fusion of EEG and peripheral physiological signals has been explored for emotion recognition. Tao et al.~\cite{tao2020} combined EEG and ECG using a hierarchical attention network for valence-arousal prediction and showed that the cross-modal attention mechanism captured physiologically meaningful brain--body relationships. However, the application of cross-attention fusion specifically to multi-level stress classification from EEG and ECG remains underexplored, which this project addresses.

\section{Transformer Architecture}
\label{sec:transformer}

The Transformer architecture~\cite{vaswani2017} has become the dominant paradigm for sequence modeling across natural language processing, computer vision, and increasingly, biosignal analysis. The core mechanism is scaled dot-product attention:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:attention}
\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{Q}$, $\mathbf{K}$, and $\mathbf{V}$ are the query, key, and value matrices, and $d_k$ is the key dimension. Multi-head attention extends this by projecting $\mathbf{Q}$, $\mathbf{K}$, and $\mathbf{V}$ into $h$ different subspaces and computing attention in parallel:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:multihead}
\text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h)\mathbf{W}^O
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{W}_i^Q, \mathbf{K}\mathbf{W}_i^K, \mathbf{V}\mathbf{W}_i^V)$ and $\mathbf{W}^O$ is the output projection. This allows different heads to capture different types of relationships in the data.

For EEG data, Transformers offer a natural modeling framework when channels or time steps are treated as tokens in a sequence. Song et al.~\cite{song2022} demonstrated that Transformers applied to EEG achieved state-of-the-art results on emotion recognition benchmarks by capturing long-range dependencies between EEG channels. Positional encoding injects sequence order information into the model, as the attention mechanism is otherwise permutation-invariant. Sinusoidal positional encoding~\cite{vaswani2017} uses:

\begin{align}
\label{eq:posenc_sin}
PE_{(pos, 2i)} &= \sin\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right) \\
\label{eq:posenc_cos}
PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} &= \cos\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)
\end{align}

\noindent where $pos$ is the position and $i$ is the dimension index.

\section{Handling Class Imbalance}
\label{sec:imbalance}

Class imbalance is a persistent challenge in physiological signal classification, where certain conditions or states are naturally more prevalent than others. Standard cross-entropy loss treats all classes equally and produces classifiers biased toward majority classes. Two principal strategies address this problem.

Cost-sensitive learning modifies the loss function to assign higher penalties for misclassifying minority classes. The weighted cross-entropy loss scales each class's contribution by an inverse-frequency weight $w_c = N / (C \cdot n_c)$, where $N$ is the total number of samples, $C$ is the number of classes, and $n_c$ is the count for class $c$.

Focal loss~\cite{lin2017} extends this by additionally down-weighting easy, well-classified examples to focus training on hard, misclassified samples:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:focal}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}} = -\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{c=1}^{C} \alpha_c (1 - p_{t,i})^{\gamma} \log(p_{t,i})
\end{equation}

\noindent where $p_{t,i}$ is the predicted probability of the true class for sample $i$, $\alpha_c$ is the class weight, and $\gamma \geq 0$ is the focusing parameter. When $\gamma = 0$, focal loss reduces to weighted cross-entropy. As $\gamma$ increases, the contribution of easy examples (high $p_t$) is increasingly suppressed, directing the model's learning effort toward difficult boundary cases.

\section{Large Language Models for Health Feedback}
\label{sec:llm}

Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4, Grok, and LLaMA have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating contextually appropriate, coherent natural language text~\cite{brown2020}. In healthcare applications, LLMs have been explored for clinical note summarization, patient communication, and health coaching~\cite{singhal2023}. The integration of an LLM at the application layer of a biosignal classification system represents a novel approach to bridging the gap between numerical model outputs and actionable health guidance. By providing the LLM with the predicted stress level, confidence score, and probability distribution, the system can generate personalized wellness recommendations that account for the severity and certainty of the assessment.

\section{Summary of Literature Review}
\label{sec:lit_summary}

The literature reviewed in this chapter establishes several key insights. First, the stress response involves coordinated brain--heart activity that motivates multimodal EEG+ECG approaches. Second, deep learning has shown strong results for both EEG and ECG stress detection individually, but cross-attention fusion for multi-level stress classification remains underexplored. Third, Transformers provide a natural framework for modeling EEG channel dependencies. Fourth, focal loss with class weights addresses the severe imbalance in stress datasets. Fifth, LLMs can translate classification outputs into actionable wellness feedback. The NeuroFusionGPT framework proposed in this project synthesizes these insights into a unified architecture.

The EEG dataset is the Bangalore EEG Epilepsy Dataset (BEED), which provides 16-channel EEG recordings. Although the BEED dataset was originally collected for epilepsy classification with four seizure-type labels, this project uses the EEG signals as an auxiliary neurological modality to enrich the stress representation. The 16 EEG channel values are treated as a sequence of tokens in the Transformer encoder. The original EEG labels are not used for the fusion target; instead, the ECG stress labels serve as the unified target for both modalities.

The ECG dataset contains 187 pre-extracted features per heartbeat record with five stress levels: 0 (Calm), 1 (Mild Stress), 2 (Moderate Stress), 3 (High Stress), and 4 (Severe Stress). These features represent a comprehensive characterization of cardiac morphology and rhythm derived from the ECG waveform.

Multimodal pairing is performed by row index alignment between the EEG and ECG datasets. This pairing approach is a known limitation, as it does not guarantee that matched records originate from the same individual or temporal window. However, it provides a proof-of-concept for the cross-attention fusion architecture, which can be applied directly when synchronized, co-registered EEG and ECG recordings from the same subjects become available.

\section{Data Preprocessing Pipeline}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

\subsection{EEG Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:eeg_preprocess}

The EEG features are loaded from CSV format with 178 columns (features) plus metadata and label columns. For the deployed fusion model, the first 16 columns represent the EEG channel values used as input. The EEG features are normalized to the $[0, 1]$ range as provided in the source dataset, and a StandardScaler is fitted on the training split for additional standardization. The scaler is saved as a serialized pickle file (\texttt{scaler\_eeg.pkl}) for consistent preprocessing during inference.

\subsection{ECG Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:ecg_preprocess}

The ECG data is loaded as a CSV file with an ID column, 187 feature columns (X1--X187 corresponding to heartbeat waveform samples), and a label column. The labels are aligned from a 1--5 scale to a 0--4 scale by subtracting 1. Features are normalized using a StandardScaler fitted exclusively on the training split to prevent data leakage:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:standard_scaling}
x_{\text{scaled}} = \frac{x - \mu_{\text{train}}}{\sigma_{\text{train}}}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mu_{\text{train}}$ and $\sigma_{\text{train}}$ are the mean and standard deviation computed from the training data. The fitted scaler is saved as \texttt{scaler\_ecg.pkl} for deployment.

\subsection{Dataset Splitting and Class Distribution}
\label{subsec:splitting}

The data is partitioned with a 90\%/10\% train/validation split using stratified sampling to preserve class distributions. A separate held-out test set is used for final evaluation. \section{NeuroFusionGPT Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

The NeuroFusionGPT model comprises four components: the EEG Transformer Encoder, the ECG MLP Encoder, the Cross-Attention Fusion module, and the Classifier Head. The total architecture contains 679,557 trainable parameters.

\subsection{EEG Transformer Encoder}
\label{subsec:eeg_encoder}

The EEG encoder treats the 16 EEG channel values as a sequence of tokens and processes them with a Transformer encoder to capture inter-channel dependencies. Each channel value $x_j$ is first projected from a scalar to a $d_{\text{model}}$-dimensional vector through a linear embedding:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eeg_embed}
\mathbf{e}_j = \mathbf{W}_{\text{emb}} x_j + \mathbf{b}_{\text{emb}}, \quad \mathbf{e}_j \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{W}_{\text{emb}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times 1}$. Sinusoidal positional encoding (Equations~\ref{eq:posenc_sin}--\ref{eq:posenc_cos}) is added to provide sequence order information, yielding input tokens $\mathbf{z}_j = \mathbf{e}_j + PE_j$.

The positional-encoded sequence is processed by a Transformer encoder consisting of 3 layers, each with 8 attention heads, a feed-forward dimension of 256, GELU activation, and dropout of 0.1. Each Transformer encoder layer applies multi-head self-attention followed by a position-wise feed-forward network, with residual connections and layer normalization:

\begin{align}
\label{eq:transformer_sa}
\mathbf{h}_{\text{attn}} &= \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{z} + \text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{z}, \mathbf{z}, \mathbf{z})) \\
\label{eq:transformer_ff}
\mathbf{h}_{\text{out}} &= \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{h}_{\text{attn}} + \text{FFN}(\mathbf{h}_{\text{attn}}))
\end{align}

The output sequence is aggregated by mean pooling across the sequence dimension and normalized by a final LayerNorm layer, producing a single 128-dimensional EEG embedding vector $\mathbf{v}_{\text{EEG}} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$.

\subsection{ECG MLP Encoder}
\label{subsec:ecg_encoder}

The ECG encoder processes the 187-dimensional ECG feature vector through a two-layer MLP with batch normalization, ReLU activation, and dropout:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ecg_enc}
\mathbf{v}_{\text{ECG}} = \text{Dropout}\left(\text{ReLU}\left(\text{BN}\left(\mathbf{W}_2 \cdot \text{Dropout}\left(\text{ReLU}\left(\text{BN}\left(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{x}_{\text{ECG}} + \mathbf{b}_1\right)\right)\right) + \mathbf{b}_2\right)\right)\right)
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{W}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{256 \times 187}$ and $\mathbf{W}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times 256}$. Batch normalization stabilizes training and acts as a regularizer. The dropout rate is 0.3 for the ECG encoder, higher than the EEG encoder's 0.1, reflecting the higher dimensionality and potential redundancy in the ECG feature space. The output is a 128-dimensional ECG embedding $\mathbf{v}_{\text{ECG}} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}$.

\subsection{Bidirectional Cross-Attention Fusion}
\label{subsec:fusion}

The cross-attention fusion module models the interactions between the EEG and ECG embeddings through two parallel multi-head attention operations. In the first, the EEG embedding queries the ECG embedding; in the second, the ECG embedding queries the EEG embedding. This bidirectional design allows the model to learn both how brain activity informs cardiac interpretation and how cardiac activity informs brain signal interpretation.

Formally, each embedding is unsqueezed to add a sequence dimension of length 1 for compatibility with the attention interface. The bidirectional attention is:

\begin{align}
\label{eq:eeg_to_ecg}
\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{EEG}} &= \text{LayerNorm}\left(\mathbf{v}_{\text{EEG}} + \text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{v}_{\text{EEG}}, \mathbf{v}_{\text{ECG}}, \mathbf{v}_{\text{ECG}})\right) \\
\label{eq:ecg_to_eeg}
\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{ECG}} &= \text{LayerNorm}\left(\mathbf{v}_{\text{ECG}} + \text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{v}_{\text{ECG}}, \mathbf{v}_{\text{EEG}}, \mathbf{v}_{\text{EEG}})\right)
\end{align}

The attended embeddings are concatenated and projected through a linear layer to produce the fused representation:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fusion_proj}
\mathbf{v}_{\text{fused}} = \mathbf{W}_{\text{fuse}}[\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{EEG}} ; \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{ECG}}] + \mathbf{b}_{\text{fuse}}, \quad \mathbf{v}_{\text{fused}} \in \mathbb{R}^{128}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{W}_{\text{fuse}} \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times 256}$. The cross-attention module uses 4 attention heads and dropout of 0.1. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} presents the complete NeuroFusionGPT architecture.

\subsection{Classifier Head}
\label{subsec:classifier}

The classifier head is a two-layer MLP that maps the 128-dimensional fused representation to 5 class logits:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:classifier}
\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{W}_3 \cdot \text{ReLU}\left(\text{Dropout}\left(\mathbf{W}_2 \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{fused}} + \mathbf{b}_2\right)\right) + \mathbf{b}_3
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{W}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{256 \times 128}$ and $\mathbf{W}_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{5 \times 256}$, with dropout rate 0.2. The softmax function converts logits to a probability distribution over the five stress classes:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:softmax}
P(y = c \mid \mathbf{x}_{\text{EEG}}, \mathbf{x}_{\text{ECG}}) = \frac{\exp(\hat{y}_c)}{\sum_{j=0}^{4} \exp(\hat{y}_j)}
\end{equation}

\section{Training Procedure}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Loss Function}
\label{subsec:loss}

Training uses focal loss (Equation~\ref{eq:focal}) with the class weights from Table~\ref{tab:class_dist} and focusing parameter $\gamma = 2.0$. This loss formulation addresses class imbalance through both the frequency-based $\alpha_c$ weights, which increase the loss contribution of rare classes, and the $(1 - p_t)^{\gamma}$ focusing term, which down-weights easy examples and directs learning toward harder boundary cases.

\subsection{Optimization}
\label{subsec:optimization}

The AdamW optimizer~\cite{loshchilov2019} is used with an initial learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-4}$ and weight decay of $0.01$. The learning rate is reduced by a factor of 0.5 when the validation macro F1-score does not improve for 5 consecutive epochs (ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler with minimum learning rate $10^{-5}$). Gradient clipping with maximum norm 1.0 prevents exploding gradients in the Transformer layers.

\subsection{Early Stopping and Model Selection}
\label{subsec:early_stopping}

Early stopping monitors the validation macro F1-score and terminates training if no improvement is observed for 10 consecutive epochs. The model checkpoint with the highest validation macro F1-score is saved and used for final test evaluation. This strategy prevents overfitting while ensuring that the selected model generalizes well across all classes, not just the majority class. Macro F1 is chosen over accuracy as the monitoring metric because it equally weights per-class performance regardless of class frequency.

\section{Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:eval_framework}

The evaluation employs multiple metrics to provide a comprehensive assessment of classification performance on the imbalanced dataset. Overall accuracy measures the proportion of correctly classified samples. Balanced accuracy computes the average of per-class recall values, giving equal weight to each class regardless of frequency. Macro F1-score computes the unweighted mean of per-class F1-scores:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:macro_f1}
\text{Macro-F1} = \frac{1}{C}\sum_{c=0}^{C-1} \frac{2 \cdot \text{Precision}_c \cdot \text{Recall}_c}{\text{Precision}_c + \text{Recall}_c}
\end{equation}

Per-class precision, recall, and F1-score are reported to identify class-specific performance patterns. The confusion matrix is visualized to reveal misclassification patterns between stress levels. ROC curves and per-class AUC values are computed using the one-versus-rest strategy.

\section{LLM Integration}
\label{sec:llm_integration}

The optional LLM feedback module uses the OpenRouter API with the Grok model (\texttt{x-ai/grok-4.1-fast}) to generate personalized wellness advice. After the NeuroFusionGPT model produces a stress prediction, the predicted label, confidence score, and full probability distribution are formatted into a structured prompt. The LLM is instructed to act as a wellness assistant, providing a brief interpretation of the result and one or two actionable stress management tips. The temperature is set to 0.7 to balance coherence and variability, and the maximum response length is 250 tokens for conciseness. The API key is stored as an environment variable and loaded via \texttt{python-dotenv}.

\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{ch:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

This project has presented NeuroFusionGPT, a multimodal deep learning framework for stress-level classification that fuses EEG and ECG signals through a bidirectional cross-attention mechanism. The framework makes four principal contributions.

The first contribution is the design and implementation of a cross-attention fusion architecture that explicitly models brain--heart coupling for stress classification. Unlike simple concatenation approaches, the bidirectional cross-attention module allows EEG and ECG embeddings to attend to each other, learning interaction patterns between cortical and cardiac stress responses. The architecture is parameter-efficient (679,557 parameters) and trains within minutes on consumer-grade GPU hardware.

The second contribution is the application of modality-specific encoders that respect the structural differences between EEG and ECG data. The Transformer encoder treats EEG channels as a sequence and captures inter-channel dependencies through self-attention, while the MLP encoder efficiently processes the high-dimensional ECG feature vector. Both encoders project into a shared 128-dimensional embedding space that enables meaningful cross-attention fusion.

The third contribution is a comprehensive training strategy for imbalanced multi-class biosignal classification. Focal loss with inverse-frequency class weights addresses both the class prevalence imbalance and the difficulty variation among samples. Early stopping on macro F1 ensures model selection optimizes for balanced per-class performance rather than overall accuracy.

The fourth contribution is the integration of an LLM feedback module that bridges the gap between machine learning predictions and actionable wellness guidance. This application-layer enhancement transforms the system from a classification tool into an end-to-end wellness assessment pipeline.

\section{Key Findings}
\label{sec:findings}

The experimental evaluation yields several key findings. The fusion model achieves 93.15\% accuracy, 91.33\% balanced accuracy, and 0.783 macro F1-score on the test set. Strong performance on the Calm (F1: 0.960), Severe Stress (F1: 0.956), and Moderate Stress (F1: 0.882) classes demonstrates that the model can reliably distinguish the most clinically distinct stress states. The lower performance on Mild Stress (F1: 0.501) and High Stress (F1: 0.614) reflects both the severe class imbalance (these classes represent 2.5\% and 0.7\% of training data) and the inherent difficulty of distinguishing adjacent stress levels from physiological signals.

The precision-recall patterns indicate that the focal loss strategy successfully encourages the model to maintain high recall for minority classes (0.815 and 0.883) at the expense of precision (0.362 and 0.470). From a health monitoring perspective, this trade-off may be appropriate: it is generally preferable to flag a non-stressed individual for follow-up (false positive) than to miss a genuinely stressed individual (false negative).

The training converged efficiently, reaching the best model at epoch 27 of 37 total epochs, with the ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler providing two learning rate reductions that facilitated continued improvement after initial convergence.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations of the current work must be acknowledged. The most significant is the modality-label mismatch: the EEG data originates from an epilepsy-focused dataset (BEED) while the classification target is derived from the ECG stress dataset. The EEG signals therefore do not directly reflect stress-induced cortical changes, and the cross-attention fusion learns associations between epilepsy-related EEG patterns and ECG stress labels paired by row index rather than true physiological co-registration. This design decision was made to demonstrate the fusion architecture's capability, with the understanding that performance would improve substantially when stress-labeled EEG data becomes available.

The row-index-based alignment between EEG and ECG datasets does not guarantee that matched records originate from the same individual, the same temporal window, or even the same experimental context. This limits the physiological interpretability of the learned cross-modal attention patterns and may introduce noise into the fusion process.

The class imbalance remains a significant challenge despite the focal loss and class weighting strategy. The High Stress class contains only 641 training samples (0.7\% of total), which is fundamentally insufficient for learning robust class boundaries in a five-class problem. The macro F1-score of 0.783 reflects this limitation, as the low F1-scores for minority classes substantially reduce the macro average.

The model evaluation is conducted on a single dataset split, and cross-validation or repeated experiments with different random seeds would provide a more robust estimate of model performance and its variance.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

Several promising directions for future research emerge from this work.

The most impactful extension would be to collect or obtain stress-labeled EEG data recorded simultaneously with ECG from the same subjects. This would eliminate the modality-label mismatch and row-index alignment limitations, enabling the cross-attention mechanism to learn true brain--heart coupling patterns during stress. Publicly available multimodal stress datasets such as WESAD (Wearable Stress and Affect Detection)~\cite{schmidt2018} provide simultaneous physiological recordings that could serve this purpose, though they may require preprocessing to align with the NeuroFusionGPT input format.

Ablation studies comparing the fusion model against EEG-only and ECG-only baselines would quantify the specific contribution of each modality and the cross-attention fusion mechanism. Corrupting one modality at test time (e.g., zeroing the EEG input) and measuring the performance degradation would further validate that the model meaningfully uses both inputs. Attention weight visualization could provide insight into which EEG channels and ECG features the cross-attention mechanism prioritizes for different stress levels.

Addressing the class imbalance through data augmentation techniques, such as SMOTE applied in the feature space or generative approaches that synthesize minority-class samples, could improve performance on the underrepresented Mild Stress and High Stress classes. Alternatively, reformulating the problem as a three-class task (Low, Medium, High stress) by merging adjacent levels could reduce the difficulty while preserving clinical utility.

Robustness evaluation across different dataset splits, cross-dataset generalization testing, and evaluation under distribution shift would strengthen confidence in the model's practical reliability. Real-time deployment on wearable devices would require optimizing the model for edge inference, potentially through quantization, pruning, or knowledge distillation.

The LLM feedback module could be enhanced with personalized longitudinal tracking, where the system monitors stress trends over time and adjusts recommendations based on the user's history, response patterns, and stated preferences. Privacy-preserving deployment using on-device LLMs rather than cloud APIs would address the sensitive nature of health-related biosignal data.

\section{Concluding Remarks}
\label{sec:concluding}

Stress is a pervasive challenge with serious health consequences, and objective, automated assessment from physiological signals represents a promising avenue for early detection and intervention. NeuroFusionGPT demonstrates that multimodal fusion of EEG and ECG through cross-attention can achieve strong stress classification performance with a compact, trainable architecture. The framework's modular design---comprising independent modality encoders, a flexible fusion mechanism, and an optional LLM application layer---provides a foundation that can be extended and adapted as better datasets and deployment platforms become available.

While the current implementation operates under the acknowledged constraint of mismatched EEG and ECG data sources, the architectural principles validated in this project---Transformer-based EEG encoding, MLP-based ECG encoding, and bidirectional cross-attention fusion---generalize directly to the ideal scenario of synchronized, stress-labeled multimodal recordings. It is the author's hope that this work contributes to the growing body of research on physiological stress monitoring and demonstrates the potential of attention-based multimodal fusion as a design paradigm for biosignal classification systems.
